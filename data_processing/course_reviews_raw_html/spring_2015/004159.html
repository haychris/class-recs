If you're thinking of taking this course, realize that this is very different from MAT215 and is on a much different level. While you probably had some sort of intuition for 215 just by virtue of taking AP Calculus, 217 is completely different on the level that it is much, much more abstract and is, of course, an introduction to algebra, which not many high schools teach students. So if you take this class, realize that you will be completely lost when Professor Sarnak talks about winding numbers and dual spaces and double duals, you will suffer through long problem sets that don't seem to make any sense, you will pull a 5-day continuous all-nighter finishing the take-home midterm, you will doubt your own intelligence and your sanity, you will see hell. Yes, you will do all of this. But you will also come back, and you will be a new person, and you will know what isomorphisms and conjugate transposes are, and you will have found a new way of thinking about life, and you will have formed friendships that last a lifetime, and if that does not turn you on, as Professor Sarnak might say, then I don't know what can.

Well, well, well...here we are at the end of MAT 217 ready to be transformed (ha!) from the wee MAT 215 children we were. Honestly, the only downside of this course is the book: my attention couldn't span (ha!) the entire chapters assigned. But it didn't matter, because Professor Sarnak (and more importantly, Gauss) were there to save the day. Oof, Sarnak is the best: he not only made all the lectures really fun and interesting, but too did something that I found somewhat surprising: covered the bases (ha) for both the theoretical and practical aspects of the subject. Most math courses seem to disregard the practical aspects, but I found that these were quite important for a first year course, which made Sarnak a very interesting teacher. Thus, the material and lectures were both incredible. As with 215, the problem sets are oftentimes challenging, but rewarding. Working with others through the night is an experience you will definitely have this semester, but one you probably won't regret either. Overall, thus, this course is a great course, especially if you have it with Sarnak, and one you should definitely take!
