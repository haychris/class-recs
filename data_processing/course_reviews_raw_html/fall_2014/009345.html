What advice would you give to another student considering taking this course?
A strong background knowledge in at least one of the sciences (especially physics or biology) is highly recommended. Apart from that, general knowledge on all of the sciences helps a lot. This class focuses on mathematical models and quantitative descriptions of natural phenomena, and not on the fundamental concepts of the sciences themselves. You will learned applied math to physics, biology, and some chemistry, not the subjects themselves. Be prepared for heavy workloads and self-learning on some unclear topics. Lab reports are intense, too, but awesome learning experiences.
Be prepared for school work unlike any you have ever seen before.

In the hours before the final problem set was due, I was camping out in Icahn with some classmates, trying to finish the assignment. It was hard. Frustrating. I scribbled down some integrals, but I wasn't really sure what was going on. The person beside me was rushing to wrap up a particularly long question. "I can't wait to be done with this problem set," someone said. We grumbled in unison and rhetorically wondered aloud why we signed up for this class in the first place. Would have had so much more free time. Would have spared ourselves for so much agony. Someone asked, "yo, are you all doing ISC next semester?" There was a short pause as we stared at each other, but the answer was a unanimous yes. We giggled at ourselves, (sorta) finished the problem set, and walked out to a beautiful December evening. The thing about ISC is that although it is difficult and time-consuming, what we've been learning is just so fascinating it draws us in for another semester. The people in it - classmates, instructors, TAs, ISC alumni - also contributes to why I enjoy the class. I learned a lot this semester, but any bit of arrogance I had walking in was neatly shattered. If this sounds a freshman year you would appreciate, give ISC a shot. Come in with a strong background in relevant skills - calculus, physics, MATLAB, java, group work - and your life will be easier. Try it out anyway even if you don't but don't be afraid to drop. Recommended with great caution.

This course is a lot of physics (and therefore a lot of math), with quantitative applications in biology (and a bit in chemistry). Try your best to start the problems sets early in the week when they're first announced so that you can actually ask the TA's questions during the Wednesday night problem sessions or go to their office hours for clarification (which you'll almost definitely need on some of the material). Also, try your very hardest (though it doesn't usually work) to not leave lab reports to the weekend before they are do; if you do this (as I often ended up doing), your weekend will be miserable and you will get very little sleep. Perhaps the material will come more easily to you / you'll be faster at working through the problem sets than I was; otherwise, you probably won't be getting a lot of sleep. Despite the heavy workload, some of the professors are wonderful, the material is generally fascinating, and you'll form strong bonds with your other classmates. Don't be afraid at the start to work together with your classmates!! And know that there ARE resources (TA's, professors, office hours, peer tutors, etc.) to help you if you are having trouble with the material; that being said, it can often be difficult to arrange your time suitably in order to take full advantage of these resources. Also, you really have to be invested and committed to take this course, as you WILL end up having to devote the majority of your academic time and efforts to it (so don't just decide to take it on a whim). On the whole, though, I feel as though this course has given me a much better understanding of what science and scientific inquiry actually involve, and has changed the way I approach scientific problem solving. Oh, and the labs are fantastic (and while the lab reports can be a pain, at least you feel as though you are doing something legitimately worthwhile and are learning clear skills by doing so)!

This course is just as demanding if not more demanding than what people say. It was definitely far more time consuming than I expected. However, the course did indeed achieve its expressed purpose of changing my perspective on science. The class really is integrated rather than multidisciplinary, and it felt as though they taught us how to think scientifically more than just content. I found this method of teaching to be particularly intelligent since learning content can be done without too much difficulty on one's own time but having one's perspective on an entire field that many of us identify with is a more rare and valuable opportunity this course provides. Very little memorization is required and what we do end up remembering is due to it coming up repeated during the course rather than deliberate rote memorization. The course content is developed very mathematically and model-orientated rather than fact or observation orientated. It definitely is a class that teaches us how to think like a scientist in that they show us the thought process behind derivations and models, and the lab portion of the course demands us write quite professional looking lab reports and give good guidance on how to do so. As a warning, the class demands a ton of work but the grading seems to be fair. I highly recommend the course for anyone interested in either becoming a scientist or learning how to think scientifically.
