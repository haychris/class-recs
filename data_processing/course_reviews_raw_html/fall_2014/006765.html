The material is very interesting, but the assignments are just done in a terrible way. It's not that they're hard, it's that they are vague, graded surprisingly harshly (as far as I can tell from the grading distributions, most people are at a B- overall, and there will not be a curve), and little feedback is provided. It's interesting, for a class where a lecture went over how important feedback is for learning, how little feedback we got. In other words, the material is great and I highly recommend reading those readings, the class is poorly executed. For Professor Shafir: you can improve your course by increasing the amount of feedback students. Even though I feel like I've learned a lot, I don't really have a benchmark to see if I really understand the material, or if my understanding is improving. Assign a few more assignments, and instead of using them to evaluate students like it seems like you are, use these assignments to actually teach people. That's what really makes a great educator, in my opinion: someone who uses assignments and tests to teach, not just to evaluate. Maybe you want to be a great researcher and not a great educator, but if so, why teach the course in the first place?
