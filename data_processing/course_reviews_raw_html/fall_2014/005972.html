This class is analogous to the popular press history books that the professor has written--superficial and sensationalist. Having taken coursework in early Christianity I was very disappointed by this class. The analysis of the canonical gospels that was presented was minimal or absent. It usually went something like "Mark uses this word and Luke uses this word--how inconsistent! Let's move on." The dating of the texts were not discussed at all, dates were just assigned and questions as to where the dates came from were not answered. The canonization process was oversimplified and often misrepresented in lecture. Pagels seemed to think that certain Church Fathers were singularly responsible for the acceptance or rejection of certain texts, in spite of them having no legitimate means of exercising power over the church. The use of specific gospel traditions in the works of the apostolic fathers was not discussed. Pagels frequently pointed out the biases of certain scholars (Schweitzer, for example) but seemed very comfortable ignoring her own. I was surprised by the derision with witch she spoke about the role of women expressed in several canonical texts. By all means she can disagree with them, but making passive aggressive statements about them sounded childish. Some of the paper topics were ridiculous. "Imagine you are making a movie about early Christianity" began one of them. I felt like I was in middle school again. Don't take this class, it is a waste of time. Just buy one of Pagels's books instead.
