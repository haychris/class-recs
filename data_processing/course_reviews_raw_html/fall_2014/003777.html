I found these reviews very helpful when I was picking courses so I will give here a lengthier review. HUM is slightly less challenging than what many of us expected. In fact, this course is indeed "what you want out of it" in that you really do have quite a bit of leeway in regards to how you spend your time doing the work, thereby making the tremendous amount of reading more manageable and interesting. For example, if you are particularly interested in philosophy, you can speed read the history and literature to get the flavor of the author and text but then slow down and digest all the philosophy, then write all of your essays on philosophy, spend time talking to the philosophy professors, etc. Thus if you're interested in either history, literature, or philosophy (or some combination but not necessarily but potentially all) then I highly recommend HUM. The course truly is about the books with the lectures, precepts, and essays as supplementary so if you have the discipline perhaps this course isn't absolutely necessary since one could simply read all the books but having the structured course and guidance was valuable. One particular benefit from reading the books in HUM I didn't realize going in was that every time we read a text by some author, we have by that point already read many of the texts that the author himself read which gave great context to understanding the text but also was enjoyable since we were following an incredible intellectual journey of western thought. Fair warning though, it seems that HUM gives out lower grades than what one would hope for, so I must say, having the perspective of taking HUM to change your view on things and introduce you to new ideas and personal improvement is definitely healthier and less stressful than focusing on grades. Highly recommended nevertheless!

If you are a humanities lover, take it!! And if you don't feel ready as a freshman, do it as a sophomore. After being super on-the-fence, I finally decided to take the class and am so glad I did. The readings are amazing and thought-provoking, and in an ideal world, every educated person would be familiar with them. The lectures are excellent, with top-notch professors who also provide insight into pedagogical differences as they each handle the texts in their own ways. The precepts are really special, with a much higher level of student engagement than in other courses I've taken here, and it's easy to make HUM friends both inside and outside the classroom. The papers are hard to write, but practicing them definitely helps you improve your essay-writing abilities in general, and the workload of the course is manageable (much more manageable than people make it out to be--don't be scared!). HUM feels like a bigger experience, something more than a class, and you get to delve into the texts to a substantial degree despite the survey nature of the course. Advice for when you take HUM: Don't fall behind on readings by more than a couple of days, or you will lose motivation to catch up and get swamped. If you can, set aside time so that you finish the reading for a particular lecture one day before that lecture, leaving yourself a little buffer room for unforeseen interruptions to your reading schedule. And definitely do as much of the readings as you can! This course revolves around the texts, and I think personally engaging with the books is key to fully enjoying lectures and precepts.
