. The first half of the course is learning various OR-type things, like economic order quantity, newsvendor problem, etc. Second half is all OJ game, plus lectures you probably will skip since they don't have much to do with the game. I think it's better if everyone on your team can at least understand what everyone else is doing. This way you can help each other out instead of sitting around wondering what to do next and you can interface the stuff you work on with everyone else's better. Inevitably though people carve out pieces of the project for themselves, and only they understand what their piece does. It's important to maintain a good team atmosphere - if your team starts doing poorly, try not to hate each other. Teams essentially maintain their original rankings. So put a lot of effort into the first year of business, because if you start behind you will never recover. Rankings change only when a team makes a serious mistake and falls behind--this is more likely to happen on OJ bowl day. So it's important to rehearse what you'll do that day, and if you decide to automate decision making and analysis procedures, check to make sure that your code is bug-free. Unfortunately there are no OJ bowl day practice runs. Many teams end up building a simulator of the actual OJ game simulator, and in fact the instructors suggest that you do so. Do so, and make good choices about what language to write it in, how to make it extensible so that it accommodates any parameters you throw at it. It's up to you how closely the simulator mimics the actual simulator. Closer is harder of course. The expressiveness of an actual programming language beats the heck out of Excel, but you will end up using a decent amount of Excel, especially since your decisions and results come in Excel files. So learn to interface programming languages with Excel.
