Professors: Koel is strict, but somewhat fair. Be sure to make your references of large font in your presentation and succinct. P'homie: Nicest of the 3 profs. Won't actively help you but more than willingly to answer your questions with straight answers if you need help understanding a concept or apparatus. Benziger: Brilliant man, but has a bit of an ego. Flatter him, and he'll help you. Ask him for help because otherwise, he'll tank you and your grade by asking you graduate-level questions far beyond your capacity. Gives absolutely useless advice and it's ironic how in his presentations, he often does the very things he says not to. Labs: Some are easy, some are hard. The hard ones are anything to do with dimensionless quantities (like heat transfer, polymer extrusion, fluidized beds). Easy ones include enzyme kinetics, control schemes (not level nor mixing but temp control), distillation, etc. It's unfair that some people will get 4 easy labs, and some people will get 4 hard labs, but sucks to suck. Try to put the minimum work on reports and posters and oral presentations since the grading is a crapshoot. Honestly, the 3 professors will collaborate to determine your oral presentation grade, which means that a professor who thinks you deserve a 95 will cross that 95 out and give you an 85 if the other 2 profs feel that way. It's a shit system. Written report: You do not have to complete every point mentioned in the handout. Rather, pick a few to focus on and tie together .The other ones, complete (with minimal effort) in the appendices. Talk to your grading professor about what to focus on and what they would like to see. Expectations vary across the 3. Oral report: Speak LOUDLY, make eye contact, and practice. 50% of your grade is based on how well you exude confidence and dominate that room. Make sure your PPT slides are spotless, because you don't want to give the profs any extra ammo for criticizing you. 50% of your grade is on your data. Like a true scientist, pick and choose your data to show only the best of the best. Otherwise, you will get screwed because what really matters in science, of course, is how well your data looks.
