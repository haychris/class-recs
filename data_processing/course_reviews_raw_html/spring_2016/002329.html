The great thing about the two semesters of diff eq offered here is that they both are taught radically differently and with different intents. Stone's section is reportedly harder and takes a lot of time but is more engaging and you learn a lot of out of it and he has support for struggling students. So if you identify learning as a spiritual experience, then maybe his class is better for you. But Yannis' class is designed for people who stick by the book, are in it to build a basic foundation, and are for people who need to just either need credit or "need to know diff eq". That might make it seem like Yannis' class is bad, but that would be hugely disrespectful to what his course offers and to Yannis himself. Yes, the teaching could be better, but this is something Yannis consciously acknowledges that he has a lot of improve on and puts in the effort to do so. He cares a lot. The book is actually great and readable, and makes a good complement to the course. The preceptors and Yannis put in a great deal in having office hours, being responsive to emails, and caring. And the fact that this course is designed to teach "practical diff eq" does not mean that you will learn nothing, and in fact I would say I learned quite a lot. The course is deliberately taught this way. So having said all of this, I was very satisfied actually by what the course offered even if at times it wasn't thrilling. But it is up to you to decide which is best for you and figure out what you want for yourself, and which learning style is "easy" or "hard".
