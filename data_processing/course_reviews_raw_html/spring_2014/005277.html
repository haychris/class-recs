Like with any other course, a recommendation to take it comes with the caveat of having some prior interest in the material. This sort of interest isn't inherently with freedom, democracy, government, and all that stuff, but rather, it is an interest towards philosophical and serious topics. People who have an idealistic view of the world, those who don't care for keeping up with politics or aren't seriously engaged with political issues, those who prefer to avoid controversy and go with the status quo, etc, will not find very much out of this course. But if you do like exploring new ideas, have a political orientation that you have reasoned, thought about, and acquired through looking at all sides of an issue and choosing one that best fits how you feel about the world, then taking this class should be a priority. In taking this class, one thing to do is to find the best translation possible. Some authors, such as Hobbes, can be undecipherable if the translation is dated, or in the case of old English, left untranslated. Other translations adhere far too much to the original wording and grammar of the authors, and this too makes them undecipherable. Don't just go to labyrinth and buy the one on the list - shop around online for a translation of the text that you like, and that you can read and understand. You'll enjoy the course a lot more because of this. Something else to keep in mind is that you won't like all the authors you read. They're all different, and they sort of have a life of their own. In such a case, you may be tempted to skip that author. Thanks to the organization of the course, that is entirely possible to do, or at least it was when I took it. I think the option to ignore authors whom you think are ridiculous and undecipherable is a very desirable trait in a course, and ought to be a feature in all courses.
