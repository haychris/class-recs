If you are interested in learning a bit about proofs and don't mind a bit of a challenge, you should definitely take 204 over 202. The class avoids many of the tedious calculations that 202 has to do, and instead we get to do fun proofs (but not too challenging).
If you are not a Math Major taking 217 or 218, but you want a little more than the rote computation of 202, than this course is for you.
If you aren't interested in pure mathematics and are just taking linear algebra as an engineering, economics, etc. requirement, I would warn you to think carefully before taking this course. Professor Taylor moves very fast during lecture (it often went over my head), and the supplementary problems on the problem sets are very time consuming without being really interesting or rewarding. Each problem set took me at least 10 hours to complete, often more, with a lot of help from other students. While I learned more in this course than I would have in 202, I don't know how necessary it really was, and my grade certainly wasn't as good as it could have been. One the other hand, the curve is much more generous than in 202 (average test score is usually just above 50%, and this is about a B+). If you do decide to take this course, here are my suggestions to do well (which I admittedly didn't usually heed myself): 1) read the textbook section before lecture. Professor Taylor moves so fast that it's good to have a basic understanding going into lecture. If you don't read it before lecture, definitely read it afterward. 2) start the problem sets early in the week. Ideally, do each book section after lecture, then do the supplementary problems over the weekend (starting saturday, not sunday). 3) find a study group! don't try to do the problem sets by yourself, even if you can manage it in the beginning, because they get more difficult as the semester progresses, and you will likely need help later on. also, working together makes the problem sets more fun

If you're worried about having a ton of work or if you're just doing linear algebra for a distribution requirement, you may not want to take it.
If you have never done proofs before, make sure you try and do homework with someone who has in the first couple of weeks. Don't be afraid to ask about concepts, as everything builds off of everything else.
