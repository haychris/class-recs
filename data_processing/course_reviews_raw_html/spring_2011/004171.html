Do not, under any circumstances, take this course. The lectures were too fast, and jumped around many different topics instead of sticking with one for more than one week. The entirety of the material is theorems and proofs--which I normally like, but in this case, they were wholly uninteresting. Absolutely no time was devoted to applications (which is surprising given the widespread use of graph theory in computer science), making everything we learned seem pretty useless. Professor Seymour was an okay lecturer--he was well-prepared and very knowledgeable about the material. It was great that he stopped every so often to ask something like "why is this true?" or "how should we start proving this?" However, this bonus to his teaching quality was offset by the fact that most of the time, the same one or students (who pretty much already knew the course material) would answer the questions, so the professor would just move on, assuming everyone else knew it. He often went to fast, and was not good at answering questions. He had no conception of the material's difficulty, and no particular regard for the students who were struggling with it. The problem sets were very long and difficult, and pretty much impossible to do without extra help from the TAs. It would have been much better to have problems which more closely follow the material from that week's lectures, and which are more doable.
Don't expect it to be very applied. Quite theoretical (all theorems and proofs), and rather challenging problem sets at times. Be prepared to spend at least 15 hours a week on the class.
