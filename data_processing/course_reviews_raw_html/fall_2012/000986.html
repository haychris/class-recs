Organic Chemistry I is a course you should seriously consider, whether or not it is required for your major. The lectures, despite the large number of students, are taught in an engaging manner, the professors, preceptors, and lab TA's are genuinely helpful, and the course is really designed to help you learn instead of "parroting" back information. The homeworks are graded on completion and the formal labs are submitted in a draft/final version process, so you can use the written as a tool to help you understand the material rather than worrying about points. The lowest score of the three class exams is also thrown out when grading, and all the exams and the final are open notes and book, so the exam pressure is also fairly low. Overall, the class does a wonderful job of being very informative without being overwhelming.

Do the reading. At times it more clearly explains something than lectures. There is little emphasis on memorization in the course, but having as full a command of "facts" as possible will help you in exams so long as you are not stuck with a fact you can not explain. Meaning - if you memorize something, understand what chemically makes sense about the given arrangement, molecule, atomic orbital, etc. While you read, I recommend that you make outlines of chapters so that you can have something of your own to study prior to practice exams. Practice exams? Yes, do them, and do them after studying as if you would study for an exam. Which means you may need to start working to an "exam-ready" state days before the actual exam. Doing practice exams without studying may help familiarize you with problems, but it may just lead to a perpetual sense of "oh, how was I supposed to know that?". The course emphasizes problem solving but at times does not offer a clear, explicit way into this way of thinking. It can be helpful to identify for yourself a methodological approach to the problems, maybe a series of questions or a schema, that can help you organize your thoughts when you see a new, seemingly undo-able situation. There are so many "exceptions" to "rules" that the instructors are wary of setting up students for future setbacks by asking them to remember a set of rules - but students may be more successful if they attempt to consciously reflect on the more schematic side of things. The students who do this really well are often unaware of what it is they are doing well -- which often creates an image of "innate" ability with orgo, but is not so much the material as an approach that they take to well. This certainly can be learned; do not be intimidated by people who may be
