I sat in on both Semmelhack's and Sorensen's course for the drop/add period and chose Sorensen for several reasons. His lectures are far more engaging (although they cover less material) and conceptually approachable. He brings a joy to the classroom that can be infectious, and students are overall more relaxed and eager to participate with questions and possible solutions to synthesis questions. That being said, the lectures rarely cover all that is in the reading, but the lectures are honed to a fine point in order to impart a conceptual grasp that allows the reading to make sense quickly. Sorensen's class rarely references the Sorrell textbook; he makes hand-written (with impeccable handwriting) sets of notes that cover everything in the course. These are incredibly easy to follow. A notable difference between the two courses, which also led me to favor Sorensen, is that Sorensen has practice exams only stretching back to 2008/2009. I found it stressful in Semmelhack's class to know that there were fifteen years' worth of practice exams that I "could" do to be more prepared (and in some sense were necessary to be "fully" prepared) for his exams. With Sorensen's old tests however, a much more manageable study schedule can be set. That being said, you may find that this doesn't provide enough practice. Sorensen's exams are bit simpler (or a lot simpler) because they are closed book with one front/back page of notes that you can bring. Since you can't consult old tests or the book or class notes, the test questions are designed to confirm fundamental knowledge and emphasize broad concepts rather than test your ability to recognize trick questions - which often seemed to be the case with Semmelhack exams. There are still "trick questions," but there is less a sense of an overabundance of material to draw on. Semmelhack's class, however, seems more interesting from an informational standpoint.
