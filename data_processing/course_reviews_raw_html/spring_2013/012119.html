Take this class for Wilcove's half, and then seriously consider dropping for Mauzerall's half. Wilcove is one of, if not the, best professors at Princeton. He's funny, he's engaging, and (most importantly) he's very nuanced. He understands that not everyone is an idealist like him, that there are pragmatic policy barriers to the ideal environmental policy solutions, and is willing to work within those boundaries to create optimal policy. He's also very open to listening to the other side -- as a conservative, I felt that anything I said would be taken under consideration fairly, although perhaps not agreed with. Mauzerall has the opposite problem. She's a very nice lady who genuinely cares for her students. But she's extremely idealistic and ignores how policy actually gets made -- her version of policy is "businesses should self-regulate themselves and be altruistic," which is nice, but not very plausible. As a conservative, I felt that anything I said would be almost attacked, so I didn't talk very much. It would have been nice to see the other perspective on climate change/ozone layer, even if she tore down the science behind the deniers -- but not even knowing what the deniers are arguing makes everyone less equipped to go into the world with a grasp of the science behind the policy. Lectures were also hard to follow at times, particularly the science. Wilcove's section was perfect. Mauzerall needs to encourage more arguments from the other side -- even if she wants to bring them up for the sake of destroying them, that would be preferable.
Talk to Mauzerall or Wilcove before taking the class, and if they seem like they have a clear direction for the course, then you should be in for an enjoyable semester. Otherwise, it might be best to avoid this class.
